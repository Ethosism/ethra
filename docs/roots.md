# Roots

Ethra roots are semantic families, not isolated meanings. A root carries a conceptual field that can generate verbs, nouns, adjectives, agents, objects, ritual forms, civic forms, intimate forms, tools, places, doctrines, disciplines, offices, records, rights, vows, and absences.

## Pattern System

For a two-consonant root such as `R-H`:

```text
rah      action/verb
reh      concept/noun
rih      quality/adjective
rahen    agent
roh      object/presence
ha-ruh   ritual form
rah-da   civic/legal form
mi-rah   intimate/emotional form
rah-ga   ongoing practice/process
rah-tel  instrument or medium
rah-wed  place or field
rah-lek  doctrine or theory
rah-lem  collective body
ne-rah   absence or deprivation
rah-gec  category or class
rah-dak  discipline or training
rah-xaf  office or mandate
rah-ket  record or archive
rah-ret  right or claim
rah-dov  vow or oath
```

For a three-consonant root such as `S-N` or future four-consonant roots, vowels are inserted between consonants to avoid clusters.

## Why Roots Matter

Roots make memory visible inside vocabulary. A speaker who hears `rah`, `reh`, `rih`, `mi-rah`, `rah-ret`, and `rah-ket` hears a family: seeing, recognition, visible truth, intimate recognition, recognition-right, and recognition-record. This creates conceptual gravity. Words become related across law, love, prayer, philosophy, technology, and public institutions.
